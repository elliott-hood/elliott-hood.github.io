1
Start with the Resources You Have
Most newsletter operators need to be scrappy. The mistake? Overextending yourself by trying to do too much with too little.
‚ùå Common Mistake
Launching 3x/week newsletter as a side project when you have a full-time job and family. Trying to send at 6am when your team doesn't start until 9am. Building a curated newsletter when you don't have staff capacity.
üí° Real Example: The New Yorker
Leadership wanted 6am send time like other newsrooms. But rigorous editing (multiple rounds + fact-checking) made this impossible without pre-dawn staffing. Plus, they published most new stories 10am-1pm. Solution: Afternoon send time that aligned with actual resources and content availability. Worked great.
‚úÖ Key Takeaway
Start weekly, then ramp up when you have time/resources. Use RSS automation if you can't curate manually yet. Focus on what you CAN do sustainably, then expand. Direction matters more than speed.
2
Keep Listening to Your Audience
How will you build a newsletter that truly meets their needs if you don't ask questions and listen to what they say?
Ways to Listen
  • Ask questions in welcome emails
  • Incorporate polls (like Financial Times does in every newsletter)
  • Run regular surveys
  • Set up 1-1 chats over Zoom or in person
üí° Real Example: Inbox Collective
Talked with 75+ readers (nearly 4% of entire list!). Started hearing from small teams without big budgets who still wanted help. Those conversations led to launching a coaching program that became one of the most reliable revenue streams.
‚úÖ Key Takeaway
The more you listen, the more you'll understand what they need. You'll also spot trends that reveal products or services you can build and sell. Make listening a regular habit, not a one-time thing.
3
Focus First on Your Flagship Product
Don't launch lots of newsletters at once. Build one flagship newsletter first, then grow from there.
The Strategy
For large brands: Build your flagship daily or Destination newsletter first. Use it to build loyal audience and set up workflow. Then launch other newsletters and promote them via welcome series, in-newsletter ads, or dedicated emails.

For independent writers: Even if you only plan one newsletter, consider launching a Course (automated email series teaching a skill/habit/lesson) to funnel readers onto your main list.
üí° Good Example: Toronto Star
Excellent at newsletter cross-promotion. Uses flagship newsletter to drive signups to secondary products. Builds main list first, then segments and expands strategically.
‚úÖ Key Takeaway
One great newsletter beats five mediocre ones. Build the flagship, get it right, then expand. Use it as the hub for growing your entire newsletter ecosystem.
4
Don't Pick a Silver Bullet Metric
Picking a single metric as your "silver bullet" is a mistake. Every metric has flaws.
‚ùå Flawed Single Metrics
Open rate: Inflated by Apple Mail Privacy Protection (2021)
List size: 10K engaged subscribers > 100K inactive ones
Click rate: Inflated by security bots (71% of clicks in Q1 2025 were bots on some platforms)
Engagement
Opens, clicks, time spent
Growth
New subscribers, churn rate
Revenue
Conversions, LTV, ARPU
Feedback
Surveys, responses, sentiment
‚úÖ Key Takeaway
Pick a handful of metrics across engagement, growth, revenue, and reader feedback. Multiple metrics give you a better chance to truly understand your audience and what they want.
5
Keep Diversifying Your Growth Strategy
Too many newsletters only focus on Owned growth (website, podcast, events). The fastest-growing newsletters look beyond this.
The Four Growth Channels
  • Owned: Website pop-ups, podcast mentions, event sign-ups
  • Earned: Partnerships, guest posting, press coverage
  • Algorithmic: SEO optimization, social media discovery
  • Paid: Ads, referral programs, giveaways
‚úÖ Key Takeaway
Don't rely on just one growth channel. If you're only using Owned tactics, you're missing opportunities. Test new channels systematically. The fastest growth comes from diversification.
6
Lean Into Your Voice with Everything You Do
Great voice in your newsletter, but generic ChatGPT copy in marketing emails? That's a problem.
‚ùå The Voice Test
Take a recent marketing email and delete every mention of your name or brand. Share it with a friend. Can they figure out who sent it? If not, you've written something generic‚Äîand generic emails rarely lead to revenue.
üí° Real Example: Tangle
Added voice and personality even to transactional messages (like renewal emails). This consistency helps keep readers paying year after year for subscriptions. Every touchpoint feels like Tangle.
‚úÖ Key Takeaway
Your voice should be everywhere: newsletters, welcome series, renewal emails, transactional messages, error pages. Infuse personality into everything. Generic is forgettable.
7
When You A/B Test, Focus on the Big Stuff
Sure, test subject lines. But focus your A/B testing on the most impactful tests: growth and revenue.
Where to Focus Testing
Growth Tests (Highest Impact):
Landing page conversion: 45% ‚Üí 50% = thousands of additional signups
Pop-up messaging and format variations
Paid ad creative and targeting

Revenue Tests (Critical):
Fundraising emails (test on 10-20% before sending to full list)
Sender name variations
Call-to-action copy and placement
Pricing and offer structures
‚úÖ Key Takeaway
Subject line tests are fine, but they only impact one day's open rate. Growth and revenue tests impact long-term success. You only get so many chances to convert readers into paying supporters‚Äîdon't waste your shot on an underperforming email.
8
Trust Is Hard to Win and Easy to Lose
Your job is to prove you're better than the average email marketer. (We've all experienced the 8 emails/day from Old Navy after buying one pair of jeans.)
How to Build Trust
  • Be clear about what they'll get and when from day one
  • Introduce yourself in your welcome series
  • If asking for data, explain why you need it (e.g., "We're planning events in different cities and need to know where you live to tell you when we'll be near you")
  • Set the bar high for content quality and clear it with every send

Readers decide who they let into their inbox and who they kick out. All it takes is ONE disappointing newsletter for them to click unsubscribe. Respect the inbox.
‚úÖ Key Takeaway
Trust is your most valuable asset. Earn it through transparency, consistency, and quality. Lose it through spam behavior, broken promises, or disappointing content. Treat every send as an opportunity to strengthen or weaken that trust.

Direction Is More Important Than Speed
The newsletter world doesn't move quickly compared to AI, search, or social media. That's generally a good thing‚Äîbut it means progress takes time.

It takes a LONG time to build an audience, build loyalty, launch new products, test monetization strategies. Good work happens in months and years, not days or weeks.
