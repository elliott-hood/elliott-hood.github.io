Most newsletters fail. Don't launch until you can answer YES to questions 1, 2, and 4, and NO to question 3:

1. Does this newsletter have a specific audience?

If your answer is "anyone and everyone!", rethink. The best newsletters are written for someone specific.

2. Do you have something unique to share?

Original reporting, unique perspective, or curated content that's difficult to find elsewhere.

3. Can that audience get similar content elsewhere?

If yes, you'll struggle. Go back to questions 1 and 2 and rethink what you're building.

4. Do you already have your own audience?

It's infinitely easier to grow if you have followers on social, a podcast, or a blog. Possible without, but expect a long road.

The Reporter

Original reporting on a beat ‚Äî local, national politics, or specific topics
Audience Size: Low thousands (local/niche) ‚Üí Hundreds of thousands (national)
Examples: The Dispatch (politics, 30k paid subs), Popular Information (accountability journalism), The Charlotte Ledger (local), HEATED (climate)

Primary Revenue

Tangle: 6,500 paid = $30k/month

Secondary Revenue

  • Advertising (especially local newsletters)
  • Local events (ticket sales + sponsorships)
Niche beats can charge premium: 340B Report charges $749/year

üí° Not Every Newsletter Needs to Make Money

Many newsletters serve personal, professional, leadership, or learning goals. Success isn't always measured in dollars.

Personal Goals Build a weekly writing habit
Professional Goals Open doors for speaking opportunities
Leadership Goals Build a space for others to learn from your experience
Learning Goals Get better at a skill through practice

Most newsletters blur between multiple categories. It's up to you to decide how to get the most out of yours.
