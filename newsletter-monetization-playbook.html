üíµ 7 Revenue Models

1. Subscriptions/Memberships

Readers pay recurring fee for access to content or community. $5/month to hundreds/year.
Build around 4 pillars:

Content

Premium articles, podcasts, newsletters

Conversation

Live interviews, Zoom Q&As, 1-to-1 access

Community

Discord, Facebook group, forums

Support

Readers just want to see you succeed

Pricing formula: Set monthly price ‚Üí Annual = Monthly √ó 10
Example: $10/month = $100/year (saves Stripe fees + encourages annual)
Platforms: Beehiiv, ConvertKit, Ghost, Substack, AWeber

2. Advertising

Hundreds of thousands per year possible. Secret: Find advertisers who add value to readers.
Ad Formats:
  • Title sponsorships
  • Native ads (in your voice)
  • Secondary ads (job listings, classifieds)
  • Banner ads
  • Dedicated emails
CPO (Cost Per Opens) Pricing:
$20-50 per 1,000 opens

Example: 10k readers √ó 50% open √ó $50 CPO
= 5,000 opens √∑ 1,000 √ó $50 = $250 per ad
Secondary ads pricing:
Native ad value √∑ 4
Example: $250 native = $65 classified
Tools: Beehiiv, ConvertKit ad networks, Paved, Passionfroot

3. Donations

Voluntary one-time or recurring support. Keep content free to all.
Trade-off: Payment is voluntary and sporadic. Expect FAR lower conversion rates and revenue than subscriptions.
Upside: Wider audience reach since content stays free.
Tools: Donorbox, Buy Me a Coffee, AWeber, ConvertKit

4. Products (Ebooks/Merch)

Package best posts into ebooks, create courses, sell merchandise (books, totes, t-shirts).
Tools: Gumroad, Podia, Etsy, AWeber, ConvertKit, Mailchimp

5. Affiliate Links

Recommend products you use. Get commission on sales you drive. Check product websites for affiliate programs.
üí° Pro tip: Sign up for Bookshop if you recommend books ‚Äî they offer 10% commission (far higher than other platforms)

6. Consulting/Teaching/Coaching

1-to-1 (consulting/coaching) or on-demand classes.
Consulting: Higher priced, more individualized, takes more time

Courses: Build once, sell in perpetuity. More upfront work, long-term revenue
Tools: Podia (host & sell courses)

7. Events/Workshops

One-off events readers pay for: digital talks, IRL conferences, private meetings, webinars.
Pricing tip: Small private group with you = expensive. Large webinar = much cheaper per person.

üìä After Launch: What to Do Next

1. Review Your Data (Track ARR)

Focus on Annual Recurring Revenue (ARR), not vanity metrics.

Example ARR calculation:
50 new subscribers √ó $100/year = $5,000 ARR this month
50 new/month for 12 months = $60,000 ARR

These subscribers renew year after year (minus churn)
  • Slow and steady growth is OK
  • Expect churn (no newsletter retains 100%)
  • If product is great, majority should renew

2. Evaluate Your Paid Offering

  • Which of the 4 pillars (content, conversation, community, support) resonate most?
  • Use engagement data + reader feedback
  • Think "add and subtract" ‚Äî pivot away from what doesn't work
  • Don't be afraid to change benefits over time
  • Announce new benefits to your audience every time you add them

3. Speak With Your Readers

  • Engage with paying audience as much as possible
  • Every email/DM is a chance to ask: What do they like? What do they want more of?
  • The more you talk with audience, the more you understand how to develop your newsletter

4. Consider Secondary Revenue Streams

  • Started with subscriptions? Maybe add ads or affiliate links
  • Don't try to do everything at once
  • Add revenue streams gradually over time

üéØ 4 Core Concepts

1. It's About Loyalty, Not Size

You don't need a massive audience to monetize, but you DO need an audience that trusts you and values your newsletter.

2. Start Small, Add Over Time

Consider a few different monetization strategies, but don't try them all at once. Start with 1-2, add more later.

3. Pick the Right Tools

Talk with your email platform about monetization features. If they can't help, look to outside vendors that integrate via Zapier.

4. Review What's Working

Make time to assess progress. If you see progress (even slow), that's a good sign. But be honest ‚Äî if something isn't working, move on.
