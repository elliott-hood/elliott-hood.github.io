Before You Launch: Complete This Brief
Most newsletter mistakes happen because people skip strategy and jump straight to tactics. Don't be like everyone else. Take time to answer these questions thoughtfully‚Äîit will save you months of wasted effort.
A
Purpose & Differentiation
Why does this newsletter need to exist?
Start with the fundamentals. If you can't clearly articulate why your newsletter exists and how it's different, readers won't sign up‚Äîand if they do, they won't stick around.
What's the purpose of your newsletter?
  • What are you trying to accomplish?
  • Why are you creating this newsletter?
  • What's the ultimate outcome you want?
What are your goals?
  • Are you building an audience for a business?
  • Are you trying to establish expertise?
  • Are you monetizing directly (paid subscriptions, ads, sponsorships)?
  • Are you driving readers to another product or service?
How will your newsletter complement or differ from newsletters that already exist?
  • What newsletters are similar to yours?
  • How is yours different?
  • What gap are you filling?
  • Why would someone choose yours over the alternatives?
B
Target Audience
Who are you writing for?
Get specific. "Everyone" is not an audience. The more clearly you can define who you're writing for, the better you can serve them.
Which audiences do you want to reach? List as many types of current or potential readers as you can.
  • Who are they? (Job titles, roles, industries, life stages)
  • What makes them unique? (Specific characteristics, challenges, situations)
  • What do they care about? (Values, priorities, aspirations)
  • Demographics: Age, location, income, education
  • Psychographics: Motivations, inspirations, behaviors
üí° Why Specificity Matters
"Marketing executives" is vague. "VP of Marketing at B2B SaaS companies with 50-200 employees struggling to prove ROI on content investments" is specific. The more specific you get, the easier it becomes to create content that resonates.
C
How You Serve Your Audience
What "job" does your newsletter do?
Think about it this way: Readers don't hire your newsletter because they want an email. They hire it because they need something done. What's that job?
How do you want to serve this audience? What do they need from you? What kinds of news, storytelling, or content are you uniquely suited to providing?

D
Success Metrics
How will you measure success?
You can't improve what you don't measure. But don't measure everything‚Äîpick the metrics that actually matter for YOUR goals.
How will you measure success for this newsletter?
Total Subscribers
How many people are on your list?
Open Rate
What % of subscribers open your emails?
Click-Through Rate
What % click links in your newsletter?
Engagement Rate
Replies, forwards, social shares
Conversion Rate
% who take desired action (purchase, sign up, etc.)
Revenue
Paid subscriptions, ads, sponsorships, product sales
Retention Rate
How many subscribers stay vs. unsubscribe?
Growth Rate
How fast is your list growing?
üéØ Pick Your North Star
Do you envision one (or several) of these as the key metric(s) for this product?

Don't try to optimize for everything. Pick 1-3 metrics that align with your goals from Section A. If your goal is monetization, revenue matters more than total subscribers. If your goal is building an audience, growth rate and engagement matter more than clicks.

Your name should:

‚Ä¢ Communicate what it's about (or intrigue people enough to click)
‚Ä¢ Not be easily confused with existing newsletters
‚Ä¢ Work well as an email subject line
‚Ä¢ Be available as a domain (if you want your own site)
üí° Frequency Considerations
Daily: High commitment, requires consistent content pipeline. Best for news/updates.
Weekly: Sweet spot for most newsletters. Enough frequency to stay top-of-mind, not so much that you burn out.
Bi-weekly/Monthly: Works for in-depth analysis or when each issue requires significant research.

Be realistic about what you can sustain. Better to be consistent at bi-weekly than to promise weekly and miss sends.
